Nutrition Knowledge Base — Sample Reference Document
=====================================================

1. MACRONUTRIENTS
-----------------
Macronutrients are the three primary energy-providing nutrients:

  Carbohydrates (4 kcal/g)
    Carbohydrates are the body's preferred energy source. They are found in
    grains, fruits, vegetables, legumes, and dairy products.
    - Simple carbohydrates (sugars): quick energy, rapid blood-sugar rise.
    - Complex carbohydrates (starches, fiber): slower digestion, sustained energy.
    - Recommended intake: 45–65% of total daily calories (Dietary Guidelines 2020-2025).

  Proteins (4 kcal/g)
    Proteins are essential for building and repairing tissues, producing enzymes
    and hormones, and supporting immune function.
    - Complete proteins (all essential amino acids): meat, poultry, fish, eggs, dairy, soy.
    - Incomplete proteins: most plant foods (beans, nuts, grains).
    - RDA: 0.8 g per kg of body weight for sedentary adults;
            1.2–2.0 g/kg for athletes.

  Fats (9 kcal/g)
    Dietary fats support cell membranes, hormone production, and fat-soluble
    vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K).
    - Unsaturated fats (olive oil, avocado, nuts): heart-healthy.
    - Saturated fats (butter, red meat): limit to <10% of daily calories.
    - Trans fats (partially hydrogenated oils): avoid; raise LDL cholesterol.
    - Omega-3 fatty acids (salmon, flaxseed, walnuts): anti-inflammatory.


2. MICRONUTRIENTS — KEY VITAMINS
---------------------------------
  Vitamin A:  Vision, immune function, skin health.  Sources: liver, carrots, sweet potato.
  Vitamin C:  Collagen synthesis, antioxidant, iron absorption.  Sources: citrus, bell pepper.
  Vitamin D:  Calcium absorption, bone health, immune modulation.  Sources: sunlight, fatty fish.
  Vitamin E:  Antioxidant protection of cell membranes.  Sources: nuts, seeds, vegetable oils.
  Vitamin K:  Blood clotting, bone metabolism.  Sources: leafy greens, fermented foods.
  B-vitamins: Energy metabolism, red blood cell formation, neurological function.
              Sources: whole grains, meat, eggs, legumes, leafy greens.


3. MICRONUTRIENTS — KEY MINERALS
----------------------------------
  Calcium    1000–1200 mg/day  Bone/teeth strength, muscle contraction.  Dairy, tofu, kale.
  Iron       8–18 mg/day       Oxygen transport (hemoglobin).            Red meat, lentils, spinach.
  Magnesium  310–420 mg/day    Muscle/nerve function, blood sugar.       Nuts, seeds, whole grains.
  Potassium  2600–3400 mg/day  Blood pressure regulation, heart rhythm.  Banana, potato, beans.
  Zinc       8–11 mg/day       Immune function, wound healing.           Meat, shellfish, legumes.
  Sodium     <2300 mg/day      Fluid balance, nerve transmission.        Table salt, processed foods.


4. HYDRATION
------------
  Water is essential for every metabolic process.
  - General recommendation: ~3.7 L/day for men, ~2.7 L/day for women (total from all sources).
  - Increase intake during exercise, hot weather, or illness.
  - Signs of dehydration: dark urine, fatigue, headache, dizziness.


5. DIETARY FIBER
----------------
  Fiber supports digestive health, lowers cholesterol, stabilizes blood sugar, and
  promotes satiety.
  - Soluble fiber (oats, legumes, apple): dissolves in water; slows digestion.
  - Insoluble fiber (wheat bran, vegetables): adds bulk; accelerates transit time.
  - Daily target: 25 g/day (women), 38 g/day (men) — most adults fall short.


6. CALORIC BALANCE & WEIGHT MANAGEMENT
---------------------------------------
  Energy balance:  Calories in vs. calories out.
  - Deficit (~500 kcal/day) → ~0.45 kg (1 lb) weight loss per week.
  - Surplus (~500 kcal/day) → ~0.45 kg weight gain per week.

  Estimated Daily Caloric Needs (Mifflin-St Jeor, moderate activity):
    Adult male 70 kg, 175 cm, 30 yr:  ~2700 kcal
    Adult female 60 kg, 165 cm, 30 yr: ~2100 kcal


7. POPULAR DIETARY PATTERNS
----------------------------
  Mediterranean Diet:
    Emphasises vegetables, fruits, whole grains, legumes, olive oil, fish.
    Associated with reduced cardiovascular disease risk.

  DASH Diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension):
    Low sodium, high in potassium, calcium, magnesium.
    Clinically proven to lower blood pressure.

  Plant-Based / Vegan Diet:
    No animal products. Requires attention to B12, iron, zinc, calcium, omega-3,
    and vitamin D supplementation.

  Ketogenic Diet:
    Very low carbohydrate (<50 g/day), high fat, moderate protein.
    Induces nutritional ketosis. Short-term weight loss; long-term effects debated.


8. PROTEIN-RICH FOODS (per 100 g, cooked)
------------------------------------------
  Chicken breast (skinless)  31 g protein  165 kcal
  Canned tuna (in water)     26 g protein  116 kcal
  Lentils (boiled)           9 g protein   116 kcal
  Eggs (whole, boiled)       13 g protein  155 kcal
  Greek yogurt (plain)       10 g protein   97 kcal
  Cottage cheese (low-fat)   11 g protein   72 kcal
  Tofu (firm)                8 g protein   76 kcal
  Black beans (boiled)       9 g protein  132 kcal


9. GLYCEMIC INDEX (GI) REFERENCE
----------------------------------
  Low GI (<55):   Most fruits, legumes, non-starchy vegetables, nuts, whole-grain pasta.
  Medium GI (55-69): Brown rice, oats, honey, whole-wheat bread.
  High GI (≥70):  White bread, white rice, potatoes, sugary drinks, cornflakes.
  Lower GI foods produce smaller, slower rises in blood glucose — beneficial for
  diabetes management and sustained energy.


10. SPECIAL NUTRITIONAL CONSIDERATIONS
----------------------------------------
  Pregnancy:
    Folate (400–800 µg/day) — prevents neural tube defects.
    Iron, calcium, DHA (omega-3) requirements increase.

  Older Adults (>65):
    Higher protein needs (1.0–1.2 g/kg) to prevent sarcopenia.
    Vitamin D + calcium supplementation commonly recommended.

  Athletes:
    Carbohydrate loading pre-event; protein post-workout for muscle repair.
    Electrolyte replacement for endurance exercise >60 min.

  Vegetarians / Vegans:
    Monitor B12 (supplement essential for vegans), iron, zinc, iodine, omega-3,
    and vitamin D.
