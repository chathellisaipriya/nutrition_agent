Indian Nutrition Knowledge Base — Extended Reference
=====================================================

1. INDIAN STAPLE FOODS — NUTRITIONAL PROFILE
─────────────────────────────────────────────
(Per 100 g cooked unless stated)

  Roti / Chapati (whole wheat): ~120 kcal, 4g protein, 20g carbs, 3g fat, 3g fiber
  Basmati rice (cooked):        ~130 kcal, 3g protein, 28g carbs, 0.3g fat
  Dal (yellow toor, cooked):    ~116 kcal, 9g protein, 20g carbs, 0.4g fat, 4g fiber
  Rajma / Kidney beans:        ~132 kcal, 9g protein, 22g carbs, 0.5g fat
  Chana (chickpeas, cooked):   ~164 kcal, 9g protein, 27g carbs, 2.6g fat, 8g fiber
  Moong dal (sprouted, raw):    ~30 kcal, 3g protein, 4g carbs, 0.2g fat per 100g
  Paneer (low-fat):             ~265 kcal, 18g protein, 3g carbs, 20g fat, 0g fiber
  Curd / Dahi (full-fat):       ~98 kcal, 3g protein, 4g carbs, 6g fat, 0g fiber
  Soya chunks (dry):            ~345 kcal, 52g protein, 33g carbs, 0.5g fat, 13g fiber
  Ragi / Finger millet flour:   ~336 kcal, 7g protein, 72g carbs, 1.5g fat, 3.6g fiber
    (excellent source of calcium ~344 mg/100 g — best plant calcium source in India)
  Jowar (sorghum flour):        ~349 kcal, 11g protein, 73g carbs, 2g fat
  Bajra (pearl millet flour):   ~361 kcal, 11g protein, 67g carbs, 5g fat, 1.2g fiber
  Poha (beaten rice):           ~130 kcal, 2g protein, 28g carbs, 0.5g fat (per 50g dry)
  Idli (steamed, 2 pieces):     ~130 kcal, 5g protein, 26g carbs, 0.3g fat
  Dosa (plain, 1 medium):       ~120 kcal, 3g protein, 20g carbs, 3g fat
  Sambar (1 cup):                ~80 kcal, 5g protein, 12g carbs, 1.5g fat


2. KEY SPICES WITH HEALTH BENEFITS
────────────────────────────────────
  Turmeric (haldi)  — anti-inflammatory (curcumin); use 1/2 tsp daily in dal/milk.
  Ginger (adrak)    — aids digestion, nausea; use fresh in tea or cooking.
  Fenugreek (methi) — lowers blood sugar (type-2 diabetes); eat leaves as sabzi
                       or soak seeds overnight and drink the water.
  Cumin (jeera)     — improves digestion, reduces bloating; 1 tsp in tadka.
  Coriander         — diuretic, rich in vitamin K; use fresh leaves as garnish.
  Amla (Indian gooseberry) — richest natural source of vitamin C (~600 mg/100g);
                             supports immunity, hair health, digestion.
  Moringa (drumstick leaves / sahjan) — extremely rich in iron, calcium, protein;
                                         excellent for anaemia and lactating mothers.


3. COMMON HEALTH CONDITIONS & INDIAN DIETARY GUIDANCE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  TYPE-2 DIABETES
  ───────────────
  • Replace white rice with brown rice, millets (bajra, jowar, ragi), or
    limit portion to 1/2 cup cooked.
  • Eat dal at every meal for protein and fibre.
  • Choose whole-wheat roti (2–3 per meal) over paratha/puri.
  • Include bitter gourd (karela) — known to lower blood sugar.
  • Avoid: maida (refined flour), mithai, fruit juices, sugary chai.
  • Glycemic Index of common Indian foods:
      White rice: GI 64–72  |  Brown rice: GI 55  |  Roti: GI 62
      Ragi roti: GI 38  |  Dal: GI 29  |  Rajma: GI 29

  HIGH BLOOD PRESSURE (HYPERTENSION)
  ───────────────────────────────────
  • Limit salt (sodium) to <2000 mg/day. Avoid papads, pickles, namkeen.
  • Eat potassium-rich foods: banana, potato (boiled), tomato, spinach.
  • Use mustard oil or olive oil instead of ghee in excess.
  • Garlic (2–3 cloves/day raw or cooked) may lower blood pressure.
  • Follow a DASH-like pattern: more vegetables, fruits, low-fat dairy, whole grains.

  ANAEMIA (IRON DEFICIENCY)
  ──────────────────────────
  • Iron-rich Indian foods: palak (spinach), methi (fenugreek), rajma, chana,
    jaggery (gud), sesame seeds (til), moringa, liver (non-veg).
  • Always pair iron-rich foods with vitamin C sources (amla, lemon, tomato)
    to increase absorption — e.g., lemon on dal.
  • Avoid tea/coffee within 1 hour of iron-rich meals (tannins inhibit absorption).
  • Cooking in iron kadhai increases iron content of food.

  PCOS (POLYCYSTIC OVARY SYNDROME)
  ─────────────────────────────────
  • Low-GI diet is key: millets, oats, whole wheat, brown rice.
  • High-protein breakfasts reduce cravings: moong dal chilla, egg bhurji, paneer.
  • Include omega-3: flaxseeds (alsi), walnuts, fatty fish (if non-veg).
  • Reduce refined carbs: maida, biscuits, white bread, packaged snacks.
  • Regular physical activity (30 min/day) alongside diet.

  OBESITY
  ───────
  • Calorie deficit of 300–500 kcal/day for sustainable weight loss.
  • High-volume, low-calorie foods: salads, soups, buttermilk (chaas), cucumber.
  • Replace evening snacks with roasted chana, makhana (fox nuts), or fruit.
  • Eat slowly, chew well, avoid eating in front of screens.
  • Avoid liquid calories: cola, packaged juices, flavoured yogurts.


4. MEAL TIMING & PORTION GUIDE (INDIAN CONTEXT)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Breakfast (7–9 AM): 25% of daily calories. Include protein + complex carbs.
    Examples: Poha with peanuts, upma, moong dal chilla, idli with sambar.
  Mid-morning (11 AM): Light snack. Fruit, handful of nuts, chaas.
  Lunch (12–2 PM): 35% of calories. Full meal: roti + dal + sabzi + curd.
  Evening (4–6 PM): Light snack. Roasted chana, makhana, tea with less sugar.
  Dinner (7–8 PM): 25% of calories. Lighter than lunch. Khichdi, soup, sabzi.
  Avoid eating after 9 PM for better digestion and sleep quality.

  PLATE METHOD (INDIAN THALI):
  Half the plate: vegetables (sabzi + salad)
  Quarter plate : grains (roti / rice)
  Quarter plate : protein (dal / paneer / curd / egg)


5. HYDRATION FOR INDIAN CLIMATE
────────────────────────────────
  • Drink 8–10 glasses (2–2.5 L) of water daily; more in summer or after exercise.
  • Coconut water (nariyal pani) is an excellent natural electrolyte drink.
  • Aam panna (raw mango drink) replenishes sodium and potassium in summer.
  • Chaas (buttermilk) — cooling, probiotic, low calorie; excellent post-lunch.
  • Avoid: packaged fruit juices, cold drinks (high sugar); opt for nimbu pani
    (lemon water) with a pinch of salt and sugar instead.


6. VEGETARIAN PROTEIN SOURCES (INDIAN)
────────────────────────────────────────
  Complete or near-complete combinations:
  • Rice + dal → complementary amino acids (complete protein)
  • Roti + dal + curd
  • Paneer (18g protein/100g) — excellent for lacto-vegetarians
  • Soya chunks — highest plant protein (52g/100g dry)
  • Greek-style hung curd (chakka) — 10g protein/100g
  • Quinoa (now available in Indian markets) — complete protein

  Daily protein target for vegetarians:
  Sedentary adult: 0.8 g/kg body weight
  Active adult   : 1.2–1.5 g/kg
  Athlete        : 1.6–2.0 g/kg


7. CALCIUM FOR VEGETARIANS (INDIAN CONTEXT)
─────────────────────────────────────────────
  Many Indians are lactose tolerant but still calcium deficient.
  • Ragi (finger millet): best non-dairy calcium source — 344 mg/100g flour.
    Make ragi roti, ragi dosa, ragi porridge.
  • Sesame seeds (til): 975 mg calcium/100g — use in chutney, laddoo.
  • Rajma, chana, soya: moderate calcium.
  • Moringa leaves: ~185 mg/100g — cook as sabzi or add to dal.
  • Curd + paneer: excellent bioavailable calcium for lacto-vegetarians.
  Adults need 1000 mg/day; postmenopausal women need 1200 mg/day.


8. VITAMIN D — A WIDESPREAD DEFICIENCY IN INDIA
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Despite abundant sunshine, urban Indians are widely deficient in vitamin D
  due to indoor lifestyles, sunscreen use, and dietary gaps.
  • Spend 15–30 minutes in morning sunlight (before 10 AM) daily.
  • Dietary sources: egg yolk, fatty fish (surmai, hilsa, rahu), fortified milk.
  • Mushrooms exposed to sunlight have some vitamin D.
  • Supplementation (600–2000 IU/day) often necessary — consult a doctor.
  • Vitamin D deficiency causes bone pain, fatigue, depression, weak immunity.
